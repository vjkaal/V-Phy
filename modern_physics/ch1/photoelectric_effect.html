Photoelectric effect
  • Photoelectric Effect
  • Compton Effect
The ejection of e-s from a material by the action of light.

A Photoelectron is the emitted electron.

Alkali metals like Li, Na, K, Rb, Cs show this effect with visible light.

Variation of Photoelectric current with intensity of light
  • It increases linearly with intensity.
  • There is no detectable time lag b/w switching on light and photoelectron emission.
Variation of photoelectric current with applied voltage

Stopping Potential: Potential just sufficient to stop photoelectric current.

  • Stopping potential is independent of incident light.
  • Max velocity of ejected e-s don't depend on intensity of incident light.
Variation of Stopping potential with frequency

Threshold frequency: The minimum frequency,vo below which there is no photoelectric current

  • Stopping potential increases linearly with the frequency.
Laws of Photoelectric Emission
  1. Photoelectric Current increases linearly with intensity of light.
  2. The max velocity of photoelectrons depend on the frequecy of incident light and not on it's intensity.
  3. Photoelectric effect don't occur below threshold frequency.
  4. The emission of photoelectrons is an instantaneous process.

Equation
When light of frequency, v shines on metal, an e- instantly absorbs a photon of energy, hv. If hv is greater than binding energy, the e- is ejected.
The photon's energy is used as:
  • The part used to release e- from the metal.
  • The remaining part appears as KE of e-.
